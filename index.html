Swar in Classical Music Swar (स्वर) refers to a musical note in Indian classical music. It is the fundamental sound used to create melodies and ragas.

Types of Swars

  • Achal Swar (अचल स्वर) – Fixed notes: Sa and Pa.
  • Vikrit Swar (विकृत स्वर) – Can be modified:
    • Komal (कोमल - Flat) – Re, Ga, Dha, Ni
    • Tivra (तीव्र - Sharp) – Ma

Alankar

Alankars can be broadly classified into four types:

Varna Alankars (वर्ण अलंकार)

Based on the movement of swars (notes). Four types:

Sthayi (स्थायी) Repetition of the same note.

Arohi (आरोही) Ascending note sequence.

Avarohi (अवरोही) Descending note sequence.

Sanchari (संचारी) Combination of all movements.

Kan Alankars (कण अलंकार)

Quick touch of one note before moving to another. Creates a graceful transition between swars. Meend Alankars (मींड अलंकार)

Smooth, gliding transition from one note to another. Gives a flowing effect to melodies. Gamak Alankars (गमक अलंकार)

Fast oscillation between notes. Used to add depth and intensity to a raga.

Raag

Raag (Raga) in Classical Music

A Raag (राग) is a central concept in Indian classical music and refers to a musical framework for improvisation and composition. It consists of a set of specific notes (swaras) arranged in a particular order, with unique melodic phrases, and is meant to evoke a certain mood or emotion. Each raag is associated with a particular time of day, season, or occasion and is designed to create an emotional impact on the listener.

Key Elements of a Raag:

Arohan and Avarohan:

  • Arohan (ascending) – The specific order in which the notes are sung or played while going up the scale.
  • Avarohan (descending) – The order in which the notes are sung or played while coming down the scale.

Vadi and Samvadi:

  • Vadi – The most important note of the raag, often referred to as the "king" of the notes.
  • Samvadi – The second most important note that complements the vadi.

Pakad:

A distinctive melodic phrase that helps identify the raag. It is a combination of specific notes played in a particular pattern.

Time and Season:

Each raag is associated with a particular time of day (morning, afternoon, evening) or season (monsoon, spring, winter). The raag is meant to capture the mood of that time/season.

Rasa (Emotion):

Each raag is meant to evoke a specific emotion or mood (e.g., joy, devotion, sadness, tranquility). For example, Raag Yaman evokes a mood of serenity, while Raag Bhairav evokes a mood of devotion or solemnity.
