Ashtanga Yoga A modern form of classical Indian yoga, popularized by K. Pattabhi Jois, involving a structured sequence of poses and breath control. Ashtanga in Patanjali's Yoga Sutras The eightfold path (Ashta = Eight, Anga = Limbs) in traditional yoga philosophy, which includes:

Yama (Ethical restraints)

Niyama (Self-discipline)

Asana (Postures)

Pranayama (Breath control)

Pratyahara (Withdrawal of senses)

Dharana (Concentration)

Dhyana (Meditation)

Samadhi (Enlightenment)

Yama in Yoga Philosophy Yama is the first limb of the eightfold path (Ashtanga) in Patanjaliâ€™s Yoga Sutras. It refers to ethical and moral guidelines that help individuals live in harmony with themselves and society. There are five Yamas, each teaching self-discipline and social responsibility:

Ahimsa (Non-violence)

Avoid harm to any living being (physically, mentally, or emotionally). Practice kindness, compassion, and patience. Satya (Truthfulness)

Be honest in thoughts, words, and actions. Speak truth in a way that does not harm others. Asteya (Non-stealing)

Do not steal or take anything that is not freely given. Avoid greed and unfair gain. Brahmacharya (Moderation / Celibacy)

Control desires and practice self-restraint. Use energy wisely, avoiding overindulgence in food, pleasures, or distractions. Aparigraha (Non-possessiveness / Non-greed)

Let go of material attachment and hoarding. Live simply and appreciate what you have. Why is Yama Important? Helps maintain inner peace and social harmony. Builds a foundation for deeper spiritual and mental growth. Prepares the mind for higher stages of yoga, like meditation and self-realization.
